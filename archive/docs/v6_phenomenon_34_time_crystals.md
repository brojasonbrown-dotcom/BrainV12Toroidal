## Abstract

[EST PHYS] Time crystals were first proposed by Frank Wilczek in 2012 as a new phase of matter that breaks time-translation symmetry in its ground state — a system that exhibits periodic motion without energy input. The initial proposal was for a continuous time crystal, but subsequent theoretical work (Bruno 2013; Watanabe & Oshikawa 2015) showed that continuous time crystals in equilibrium are impossible. The concept was rescued by the idea of discrete time crystals (Sacha 2015; Khemani et al. 2016; Else et al. 2016), which exist in periodically driven (Floquet) systems and exhibit subharmonic oscillation at a frequency that is a rational fraction of the drive frequency. [OBSERVED] The first experimental realizations of discrete time crystals were reported in 2017 by two independent groups: Zhang et al. (Google/Stanford) used a chain of trapped ytterbium ions, and Choi et al. (Harvard) used nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers in diamond. Subsequent experiments have observed time crystalline behavior in ultracold atoms, superfluid helium-3, quantum processors, and other platforms. [EST PHYS] The defining property of a discrete time crystal is the spontaneous breaking of discrete time-translation symmetry: the system oscillates with period $nT$ (where $n$ is an integer, typically 2) even though the Hamiltonian has period $T$. This is analogous to a spatial crystal, which breaks continuous spatial translation symmetry by forming a periodic lattice. [PHIL] Within the RHUFT mathematical ontology, time crystals are interpreted as systems in which the geometric field has achieved a periodic closure in the time-complexity dimension. The system's return to its geometric state after a discrete number of recursive steps is the temporal analogue of the spatial crystal's return to its lattice state after a discrete translation. [SPEC] The RHUFT claim that the discrete time crystal's period is a multiple of the drive period in a "geometric sense" is a philosophical interpretation, not a physical prediction. The period doubling observed in experiments is explained by Floquet theory and many-body localization, not by a geometric field theory.

The subharmonic response must be robust against perturbations (disorder, thermal fluctuations, changes in drive parameters) and must persist in the long-time limit. [OBSERVED] The Harvard NV-center experiment (Choi et al. 2017) used a diamond sample with $10^{16}$ NV centers, driven by microwave pulses. The magnetization was measured as a function of time, and a clear $f/2$ peak was observed in the Fourier spectrum. The peak was robust against changes in the drive amplitude and the orientation of the external magnetic field. The Google/Stanford ion trap experiment (Zhang et al. 2017) used a chain of 10 ytterbium ions, driven by a sequence of laser pulses. The spin correlations were measured, and the system exhibited period-doubling that persisted for hundreds of drive cycles.
