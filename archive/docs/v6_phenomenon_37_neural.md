## 1. The Brain as a Biological Structure: What Neuroscience Knows

### 1.1 Cellular Architecture

[OBSERVED] The adult human brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons (Azevedo et al., 2009, *Journal of Comparative Neurology*) and roughly 85–100 billion glial cells. These neurons form an estimated 100–500 trillion synapses. [EST PHYS] The cerebral cortex, a 2–4 mm thick sheet of neural tissue with a surface area of approximately 2,500 cm² (when unfolded), is organized into six layers in most regions. Each cubic millimeter of cortical tissue contains approximately 10^5 neurons and 10^9 synapses. [EST PHYS] Neurons are not simple switches; they are complex electrochemical devices. The resting membrane potential (~-70 mV) is maintained by ion pumps (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) that consume approximately 20% of the brain's total energy budget. Action potentials are generated by voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels, with typical conduction velocities of 1–100 m/s depending on axon myelination. [OBSERVED] Synaptic transmission involves neurotransmitter release (glutamate for excitation, GABA for inhibition, and dozens of neuromodulators), receptor binding, and postsynaptic potential generation. The diversity of synaptic types, receptor subtypes, and neuromodulatory states creates an astronomical combinatorial space of possible brain states.

[EST PHYS] The brain's structural connectivity is not random. It follows a "small-world" topology: most neurons connect to nearby neighbors (local clustering), but a small fraction of long-range connections (myelinated white matter tracts) create short path lengths between any two neurons. [OBSERVED] The total length of myelinated nerve fibers in the human brain is approximately 150,000–180,000 km—enough to wrap around the Earth's equator 4–5 times. This structural organization is not arbitrary; it is the product of 3.5 billion years of evolutionary optimization for information processing, energy efficiency, and behavioral control.

### 1.2 Neural Oscillations: The Five Established Bands

[EST PHYS] Neural oscillations are rhythmic fluctuations in the electrical activity of neuronal populations, observable in EEG, MEG, and local field potential (LFP) recordings. They are not epiphenomena; they are functional mechanisms that coordinate neural activity across spatial and temporal scales. The five canonical bands are:

[EST PHYS] **Delta (0.5–4 Hz):** These slow oscillations dominate during deep, dreamless sleep (slow-wave sleep, SWS) and are generated by thalamocortical circuits. Delta waves are associated with synaptic downscaling (the weakening of synaptic strength during sleep), memory consolidation, and the glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste. [OBSERVED] In deep sleep, delta power can reach 100–200 μV in frontal EEG channels. The cellular mechanism involves the slow oscillation (<1 Hz) of cortical pyramidal neurons, which synchronizes thalamocortical neurons into the delta band.

[EST PHYS] **Theta (4–8 Hz):** Theta oscillations are prominent in the hippocampus during spatial navigation and memory encoding, and in the neocortex during REM sleep and relaxed wakefulness. [OBSERVED] The hippocampal theta rhythm in rodents is generated by a circuit involving the medial septum, GABAergic interneurons, and pyramidal cells, with a frequency typically 4–12 Hz. In humans, the neocortical theta band is 4–8 Hz. Theta is associated with memory encoding, emotional processing, and the integration of information across cortical regions. [EST PHYS] The phase of theta oscillations modulates the amplitude of gamma oscillations (theta-gamma coupling), a mechanism proposed for the multiplexing of multiple items in working memory.

[EST PHYS] **Alpha (8–13 Hz):** The alpha rhythm was the first brain wave discovered, by Hans Berger in 1924. It is most prominent over the occipital cortex during eyes-closed relaxed wakefulness and attenuates with visual attention. [OBSERVED] The alpha rhythm is generated by thalamic pacemaker cells—specifically, thalamocortical relay neurons in the lateral geniculate nucleus and pulvinar—interacting with cortical pyramidal cells. T-type calcium channels in thalamic neurons generate low-threshold calcium spikes that initiate burst firing at alpha frequencies. [EST PHYS] The alpha rhythm is not merely an "idling" rhythm (as Berger initially thought); it actively suppresses irrelevant visual processing and gates information flow to the cortex. [OBSERVED] The peak alpha frequency varies across individuals (typically 8–13 Hz, with a mean near 10 Hz) and decreases with age. Alpha power is lateralized during cognitive tasks, with decreases in the hemisphere contralateral to the attended stimulus.

[EST PHYS] **Beta (13–30 Hz):** Beta oscillations are associated with motor control, sensorimotor integration, and active cognitive states. [OBSERVED] In the motor cortex, beta activity is suppressed during movement execution (event-related desynchronization) and rebounds after movement cessation (post-movement beta rebound). [EST PHYS] Beta is thought to maintain the status quo of sensorimotor circuits—"holding" a motor set or suppressing unnecessary movement. Dysregulated beta is associated with Parkinson's disease tremor and bradykinesia.

[EST PHYS] **Gamma (30–100 Hz):** Gamma oscillations are the fastest observable brain rhythms and are associated with perceptual binding, attention, and conscious awareness. [OBSERVED] Gamma is generated by fast-spiking parvalbumin-positive (PV+) GABAergic interneurons, which provide strong perisomatic inhibition to pyramidal cells. The synchronization of these interneurons at gamma frequencies creates a temporal window (~25 ms per cycle) during which pyramidal cells can fire effectively. [EST PHYS] The "binding problem" of consciousness—how the brain integrates distributed features (color, motion, shape) into a unified percept—has been proposed to be solved by gamma-band synchronization. [OBSERVED] However, gamma is also contaminated by muscle artifacts (electromyography, EMG) and miniature eye movements, making its interpretation in non-invasive EEG challenging. The 40 Hz gamma band has received particular attention since the 1990s as a potential "consciousness frequency," though this view is now considered oversimplified.

[OBSERVED] This mechanism may allow the brain to represent multiple items in working memory: each item is encoded by a gamma burst, and different items are assigned to different theta phases. ## 2. Epistemology of Measurement: How Do We Know What the Brain Is Doing?

### 2.1 EEG: An Indirect and Incomplete Window

[EST PHYS] The electroencephalogram (EEG) measures electrical potentials at the scalp, which are the summed, volume-conducted activity of millions of neurons. [OBSERVED] The signal at any electrode is a spatially and temporally filtered version of the underlying cortical activity. High-frequency signals (>100 Hz) are severely attenuated by the skull and scalp; low-frequency signals (<1 Hz) are contaminated by electrode drift and movement artifacts. [EST PHYS] Source localization—the attempt to determine which brain regions generated a given scalp potential—is mathematically ill-posed. There are infinitely many possible source configurations that produce the same scalp potential. Inverse solutions require regularization, prior assumptions, and model constraints. [PHIL] What we call an "alpha wave" or "gamma burst" is not a direct observation of neural activity; it is a statistical construct extracted from noisy, filtered, averaged data. The brain wave bands themselves are conventions: delta/theta/alpha/beta/gamma were defined by historical precedent and clinical utility, not by any fundamental biological or physical principle. [OBSERVED] Some researchers argue for additional bands (sigma, 12–16 Hz, associated with sleep spindles; high-gamma, >100 Hz; and infraslow oscillations, <0.1 Hz). The boundaries are arbitrary.

### 2.2 The Problem of Intracranial vs. Scalp Recordings

[OBSERVED] Intracranial EEG (iEEG, electrocorticography) and local field potential (LFP) recordings from animals provide much higher spatial resolution and frequency bandwidth than scalp EEG. [EST PHYS] But even these recordings are incomplete: they sample from a tiny fraction of the brain's neurons, and the recorded signal depends on the electrode size, orientation, and impedance. [OBSERVED] A typical Utah array used in primate neuroscience has 96 electrodes covering 4 × 4 mm of cortex—approximately 0.001% of the cortical surface. [PHIL] The claim that we have "measured" brain waves is therefore an approximation. We have measured voltage fluctuations at specific locations under specific conditions, and we have inferred that these fluctuations reflect coordinated neural activity. The inference is well-supported by decades of research, but it is an inference nonetheless. [EST PHYS] The advent of high-density neural probes (e.g., Neuropixels, 960 sites on a single shank) and optical imaging (two-photon calcium imaging, voltage imaging) is improving spatial resolution, but the fundamental problem remains: we cannot measure all neurons simultaneously with perfect fidelity.
