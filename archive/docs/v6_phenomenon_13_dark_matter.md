## 1. Epistemology of Measurement

### How Do We Know Dark Matter Exists?

[EST PHYS] The evidence for dark matter is multi-modal and converges from independent observational channels:

**(1) Galaxy Rotation Curves:** [OBSERVED] Spiral galaxies rotate such that the orbital velocity v(r) remains approximately flat (v ~ constant) at large radii, rather than declining as v ~ r^{-1/2} as predicted by Keplerian dynamics with only the observed luminous matter. The flatness extends to radii where the luminous matter density is negligible, implying additional mass. The velocity profiles are measured via Doppler shift of HI (21-cm) emission lines, CO molecular lines, and optical emission lines from HII regions. The rotation curves of hundreds of galaxies (e.g., THINGS survey, SPARC sample) show that the ratio of dynamical mass to luminous mass varies from galaxy to galaxy but is always greater than 1, typically by factors of 5-10 at the outermost radii.

[OBSERVED] The SPARC sample (Lelli et al., 2016) contains 175 galaxies with rotation curves, surface brightness profiles, and distance estimates. The baryonic Tully-Fisher relation (BTFR) shows that the total baryonic mass M_bary (stars + gas) correlates with the asymptotic rotation velocity V_f as M_bary ~ V_f^4, with remarkably small scatter (~0.3 dex). This tight correlation is difficult to explain with MOND alone unless the acceleration constant a_0 is fine-tuned, and it is a natural prediction of dark matter models where the dark matter halo properties correlate with baryonic properties through galaxy formation physics.

**(2) Gravitational Lensing:** [OBSERVED] Gravitational lensing by galaxies and clusters directly measures the total mass distribution, independent of dynamical assumptions. The Einstein radius θ_E = 4π (σ²/c²) (D_ls / D_s), where σ is the velocity dispersion, measures the projected mass within the lensing radius. Strong lensing (multiple images, Einstein rings) and weak lensing (shear maps of background galaxies) both find mass-to-light ratios M/L ~ 100-300 M_☉/L_☉ in clusters, far exceeding the baryonic value of ~1-5 M_☉/L_☉.

[OBSERVED] The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558, Clowe et al., 2006) is the most iconic dark matter evidence. A galaxy cluster collision has separated the baryonic matter (hot gas, observed in X-rays by Chandra) from the gravitational mass (observed via weak lensing). The gravitational potential is centered on the galaxies (which are collisionless), while the hot gas (which interacts via electromagnetic forces) is lagging behind. This spatial separation definitively demonstrates that the gravitational mass is not the baryonic mass — the dark matter is collisionless, like the galaxies, and has passed through the collision with the galaxies. [DISPROV] Any theory of modified gravity (MOND, RHUFT coherence-modified gravity, etc.) that depends only on the baryonic mass distribution would predict the gravitational potential to be centered on the hot gas, which is not observed. The Bullet Cluster is therefore a direct falsification of purely baryonic modified gravity theories.

**(3) Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB):** [OBSERVED] The angular power spectrum of the CMB temperature anisotropies has acoustic peaks at ℓ ≈ 220, 540, 810, etc. The positions and heights of these peaks are sensitive to the total matter density Ω_m h² and the baryon density Ω_b h². The Planck 2018 data gives Ω_m h² = 0.1424 ± 0.0011 and Ω_b h² = 0.0224 ± 0.0001. The ratio Ω_b / Ω_m ≈ 0.156 implies that baryons are only ~16% of the total matter. The CMB anisotropies are a snapshot of the universe at recombination (z ≈ 1100), and the acoustic oscillations in the photon-baryon fluid are damped by the photon diffusion (Silk damping) on small scales. The dark matter does not interact with photons, so it provides the gravitational potential wells that seed the acoustic oscillations without being damped. [DISPROV] MOND cannot explain the CMB acoustic peaks because it lacks the collisionless matter component that drives the oscillations. Any RHUFT-based gravitational modification would face the same problem unless it postulates an additional dark matter-like component, which would be indistinguishable from dark matter.

**(4) Large-Scale Structure:** [OBSERVED] The power spectrum of galaxy clustering (measured by Sloan Digital Sky Survey, 2dFGRS, DESI, etc.) shows a characteristic "baryon acoustic oscillation" (BAO) peak at a scale of ~150 Mpc comoving. The shape of the power spectrum is sensitive to the matter density and the growth of structure. The observed structure requires a collisionless matter component that clusters gravitationally on all scales (unlike baryons, which are pressure-supported and do not cluster on small scales before recombination). The power spectrum shape cannot be reproduced with baryons alone or with MOND alone.

**(5) Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN):** [OBSERVED] The primordial abundances of light elements (H, He, Li, D) are predicted by BBN as a function of the baryon density Ω_b h². The observed deuterium abundance (D/H ~ 2.6 × 10^{-5}) and helium abundance (Y_p ~ 0.245) give Ω_b h² ≈ 0.022, consistent with the CMB value. But the total matter density required for structure formation (Ω_m ≈ 0.31) is much larger. This is independent evidence that most matter is non-baryonic.

### The Limits of Knowledge

[PHIL] The dark matter evidence is indirect — we infer its existence from gravitational effects, not from direct detection. This is not a weakness: we infer the existence of black holes from gravitational effects, and we infer the existence of the Earth's core from seismic waves. However, the indirect nature of the evidence means that alternative explanations (modified gravity, geometric field effects, etc.) are logically possible, as long as they reproduce all of the observations. The Bullet Cluster is particularly constraining because it requires a collisionless mass component that is not co-located with the baryonic mass. Any alternative theory must explain this spatial separation.

[EST PHYS] The direct detection of dark matter particles is an ongoing experimental program: WIMP searches (XENON, LUX, LZ, PandaX) use liquid xenon time-projection chambers to detect nuclear recoils from WIMP collisions; axion searches (ADMX, CAPP, MADMAX) use microwave cavities and haloscopes to detect axion-photon conversion; sterile neutrino searches (X-ray line searches, IceCube, KATRIN) look for X-ray lines from sterile neutrino decay or missing energy in beta decay. As of 2026, no definitive detection has been reported, but the parameter space is being systematically explored. The absence of a detection does not disprove dark matter — it constrains the properties of the dark matter particle (if it is a particle).

[PHIL] **Theory-ladenness of observation:** The inference of dark matter from rotation curves depends on the assumption that Newtonian gravity (or general relativity) is correct at galactic scales. If gravity were modified at low accelerations, the rotation curves could be explained without dark matter. However, the Bullet Cluster, CMB, and large-scale structure evidence would still require an additional explanation. The current observational consensus is that dark matter is the simplest explanation that accounts for all observations, but "simplest" is a subjective criterion (Occam's razor), not a scientific proof.

## 7. The Established Physics of Dark Matter

### Dark Matter Candidates

[EST PHYS] The leading dark matter candidates:

**(1) WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles):** m ~ 10 GeV - 1 TeV, interacting via the weak force and gravity. The "WIMP miracle" is the coincidence that a thermal relic with weak-scale cross-section has the correct relic density (Ω_cdm h² ≈ 0.12) to explain dark matter. Direct detection experiments (XENON1T, LUX, LZ, PandaX) place stringent limits on the spin-independent WIMP-nucleon cross-section: σ_SI < 10^{-48} cm² for m ~ 50 GeV (as of 2024). The parameter space is being pushed to the "neutrino floor" (where backgrounds from coherent neutrino-nucleus scattering dominate).

**(2) Axions:** m ~ 10^{-6} - 10^{-2} eV, pseudoscalar particles introduced by Peccei and Quinn (1977) to solve the strong CP problem. Axions interact with photons via the aγγ coupling, and haloscopes (ADMX, CAPP, MADMAX) search for the resonant conversion of axions to photons in a magnetic field. The QCD axion mass is related to the decay constant f_a by m_a ≈ 6 × 10^{-6} eV (10^{12} GeV / f_a). The axion dark matter density is produced by the misalignment mechanism: the axion field oscillates around the minimum of its potential after the QCD phase transition, and these oscillations behave like cold dark matter.

**(3) Sterile Neutrinos:** m ~ keV - GeV, right-handed neutrinos that do not interact via the weak force (only gravity, and possibly a new gauge interaction). keV-scale sterile neutrinos are warm dark matter candidates. Their decay ν_s → ν_a + γ (where ν_a is an active neutrino) would produce a mono-energetic X-ray line at E_γ = m_s/2. Searches for this line (e.g., in Chandra and XMM-Newton observations of the Bullet Cluster, M31, and the Milky Way) have placed constraints but have not definitively detected it. The 3.5 keV line (Bulbul et al., 2014) was a tentative detection but is now considered likely to be an atomic transition (K XVIII) or instrumental artifact.

**(4) Primordial Black Holes (PBHs):** m ~ 10^{-16} M_☉ - 10^5 M_☉, black holes formed in the early universe from density fluctuations. PBHs are constrained by microlensing (MACHO, EROS, OGLE), gravitational waves (LIGO/Virgo binary black hole mergers), and CMB spectral distortions (μ and y distortions from accretion onto PBHs). The current constraints exclude PBHs as the dominant dark matter component for most mass ranges, except possibly the "asteroid mass" range (~10^{-16} - 10^{-12} M_☉) and the "stellar mass" range (~10 - 100 M_☉), though the latter is in tension with LIGO/Virgo merger rates.

**(5) Self-Interacting Dark Matter (SIDM):** Dark matter with a self-interaction cross-section σ/m ~ 0.1 - 10 cm²/g. SIDM can solve the cusp-core problem (the central density of dwarf galaxies is flatter than NFW) and the "too big to fail" problem (the most massive subhalos in CDM simulations are too dense to host the observed Milky Way satellites). Constraints from the Bullet Cluster (σ/m < 1.25 cm²/g) and galaxy cluster mergers limit the self-interaction strength but do not rule it out.

[EST PHYS] MOND (Modified Newtonian Dynamics): μ(a/a_0) g = g_N, where μ(x) → x for x << 1 and μ(x) → 1 for x >> 1. The function μ(x) is typically taken as μ(x) = x / (1 + x²)^{1/2} or μ(x) = x / (1 + x). MOND successfully reproduces the Tully-Fisher relation and the flat rotation curves of individual galaxies. However, [DISPROV] MOND fails for galaxy clusters (the observed lensing mass exceeds the MOND prediction by a factor of ~2-3), the CMB (MOND requires additional dark fields to explain the acoustic peaks), and the large-scale structure (MOND's growth of structure is inconsistent with observations). Relativistic extensions of MOND (TeVeS, BIMOND, etc.) attempt to address these issues but introduce additional fields and free parameters, undermining MOND's original simplicity.
