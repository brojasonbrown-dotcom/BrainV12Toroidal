## 1. Epistemology of Measurement

[EST PHYS] How do we measure thrust? The standard method is the use of a torsion balance or pendulum thrust stand. A torsion balance suspends the thruster from a thin fiber and measures the angular deflection caused by thrust. The calibration is performed using known forces (e.g., electrostatic actuators or calibrated weights). The sensitivity of modern torsion balances can reach micronewton levels, sufficient for measuring ion drive thrust. [EST PHYS] However, thrust measurement is fraught with systematic errors. Thermal expansion of the thruster and support structure can produce apparent thrust as the center of mass shifts. Electromagnetic forces between the thruster and the vacuum chamber walls can create spurious forces. Outgassing from heated surfaces can produce gas-dynamic thrust. Cable flexure and electromagnetic interactions with power cables can transmit forces from the ground support equipment to the thruster. The measurement of small thrusts (micro-newtons to milli-newtons) requires careful thermal and electromagnetic shielding, and the results must be reproduced in independent laboratories with different apparatus before they can be considered reliable.

[EST PHYS] The EM drive claims are a case study in the difficulty of measuring small forces. The NASA Eagleworks team (White et al. 2016) reported thrust measurements of 1.2 ± 0.1 mN/kW for an EM drive device. However, the experiment was not performed in a hard vacuum, and the team acknowledged that thermal effects were a significant concern. The Dresden University team (Tajmar et al. 2018) performed tests in a vacuum chamber and found thrust consistent with experimental artifacts. Tajmar et al. (2021) performed further tests with improved apparatus and concluded that the measured forces were consistent with electromagnetic interaction with the Earth's magnetic field and cable forces. [DISPROV] No peer-reviewed paper has established a thrust measurement for the EM drive that is inconsistent with experimental artifacts. The claim that the EM drive produces net thrust without propellant expulsion is not supported by reliable experimental evidence.

[PHIL] The theory-ladenness of observation is particularly acute in propulsion physics. When we measure a force on a thrust stand, we interpret it as "thrust" — the reaction to momentum expulsion. But if the force is actually due to thermal expansion, we are misinterpreting the data. The EM drive controversy illustrates how theoretical commitments shape the interpretation of measurements. Proponents of the EM drive interpret small forces as evidence for a new propulsion mechanism; skeptics interpret the same forces as evidence of experimental error. The measurements are the same; the interpretations differ. This is not a failure of physics but a reminder that all observation is interpretation.

## 2. Incommensurability and Friction

[EST PHYS] In a chemical rocket, the propellant is expelled at high velocity relative to the rocket. The efficiency of the rocket is characterized by the specific impulse $I_{sp} = v_e / g_0$, where $v_e$ is the exhaust velocity and $g_0$ is standard gravity. The rocket equation (Tsiolkovsky 1903) states that the velocity change of the rocket is $\Delta v = v_e \ln(m_0 / m_f)$, where $m_0$ is the initial mass and $m_f$ is the final mass. [MATH FACT] The logarithm in the rocket equation is the mathematical expression of the incommensurability between the propellant mass and the rocket velocity. The mass ratio $m_0/m_f$ is a rational number (the ratio of two masses), but the velocity change is proportional to the logarithm of this ratio, which is generally irrational. The incommensurability between the discrete mass of the propellant and the continuous velocity change creates the "friction" of propulsion: the rocket can never achieve perfect efficiency because the mass must be expelled in discrete packets, while the velocity change is continuous.

[PHIL] In RHUFT, this incommensurability is the fundamental source of propulsion's "cost." The geometric field seeks perfect closure, but the expulsion of mass creates an irreversible geometric asymmetry. The field cannot restore perfect symmetry because the expelled mass is no longer part of the system. The "friction" of propulsion is the geometric friction of incommensurability: the continuous velocity change is incommensurable with the discrete mass expulsion, and this misalignment creates a permanent geometric distortion that we call "exhausted propellant" or "entropy." The rocket's trail of hot gas is the geometric record of the incommensurability between the rocket's motion and the propellant's expulsion.

[EST PHYS] The incommensurability in propulsion is also seen in the difference between the exhaust velocity and the rocket's velocity. For a rocket to be efficient, the exhaust velocity should be matched to the mission requirements. If the exhaust velocity is too high, the rocket wastes energy accelerating propellant that it doesn't need to carry; if too low, the mass ratio becomes prohibitively large. The optimal exhaust velocity is incommensurable with the mission $\Delta v$ in almost all cases, requiring staging (where the mass ratio is reset by discarding empty tanks) to achieve the required velocity. [PHIL] Staging is the engineering solution to the geometric incommensurability. By discarding the empty stage, the rocket resets its geometric closure at a new mass ratio, allowing it to approach the optimal velocity increment in a sequence of discrete steps rather than a single continuous burn. The sequence of stages is the geometric approximation of the continuous optimal curve, just as the convergents of a continued fraction approximate an irrational number.

[DISPROV] The EM drive claims to bypass this incommensurability by producing thrust without propellant expulsion. If true, this would violate the conservation of momentum, which is a consequence of Noether's theorem applied to spatial translation symmetry. The conservation of momentum is not an empirical generalization that might have exceptions; it is a mathematical theorem derived from the structure of physical law. [EST PHYS] Any device that produces net thrust without expelling mass or emitting radiation (photons, which carry momentum) would violate the conservation of momentum and would require a fundamental revision of physics.
