## 1. Epistemology of Measurement

### How Do We Know the Baryon Asymmetry Exists?

[OBSERVED] The baryon asymmetry is inferred from multiple independent observations: (1) the CMB temperature anisotropies, which are sensitive to the baryon density Ω_b h² ≈ 0.0224 (Planck 2018), (2) the primordial abundances of light elements from Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN), which depend on the baryon-to-photon ratio η, (3) the large-scale structure of galaxy clustering, which is sensitive to the baryon fraction, and (4) the direct counting of baryons in galaxies and clusters (though this is less precise). The most precise measurement comes from the CMB: η = (6.12 ± 0.04) × 10⁻¹⁰ (Planck 2018). The BBN measurement gives a consistent value: η = (5.8-6.5) × 10⁻¹⁰, depending on the deuterium and helium abundance measurements (Cyburt et al., 2016; Fields et al., 2020). The concordance between CMB and BBN is one of the most remarkable successes of the Big Bang model.

[OBSERVED] The absence of antimatter in the universe is also inferred from: (1) the lack of antimatter cosmic rays (except for a small fraction of secondary antiparticles produced by high-energy collisions), (2) the lack of gamma-ray emission from matter-antimatter annihilation in the intergalactic medium, (3) the isotopic composition of cosmic rays, which is consistent with matter-dominated nucleosynthesis, and (4) the isotropy of the CMB, which would be affected by large-scale matter-antimatter annihilation. The upper limits on antimatter in the universe are extreme: the antimatter-to-matter ratio is < 10⁻¹⁵ on cosmological scales (Steigman, 1976; Cohen et al., 1998). The universe is overwhelmingly matter-dominated.

[PHIL] **Theory-ladenness of observation:** The inference of the baryon asymmetry depends on the Big Bang model. If the universe were steady-state, the baryon asymmetry would not be a meaningful concept (there would be no "primordial" asymmetry to measure). The measurement of η from the CMB depends on the assumption that the CMB is a relic of the Big Bang, that the acoustic peaks are due to photon-baryon oscillations, and that the cosmological parameters are constant in time. The BBN measurement depends on the nuclear reaction rates, which are measured in laboratories and extrapolated to the early universe. Both measurements are theory-laden, but they are consistent with each other and with many other observations, providing strong evidence for the baryon asymmetry.

[EST PHYS] **Calibration chains and systematic errors:** The CMB measurement of η depends on the calibration of the Planck satellite's detectors, the foreground subtraction, and the cosmological model assumptions (flatness, ΛCDM, inflation). The BBN measurement depends on the nuclear reaction rates (measured at accelerator facilities like NACRE, REACLIB, and STARLIB), the primordial abundance measurements (deuterium from damped Lyman-α systems, helium from HII regions), and the cosmological model (standard expansion history, no late entropy production). Systematic errors in the CMB include: beam shape uncertainties, detector gain drifts, foreground subtraction residuals, and polarization leakage. Systematic errors in BBN include: nuclear reaction rate uncertainties, stellar evolution corrections for helium abundances, and chemical evolution corrections for deuterium abundances. The agreement between CMB and BBN within ~2% suggests that systematic errors are well-controlled, but the possibility of unknown systematics cannot be entirely ruled out.

## 7. The Established Physics of Baryogenesis

### Sakharov Conditions and the SM

[EST PHYS] The Sakharov conditions (Sakharov, 1967) for baryogenesis are: (1) baryon number violation (B violation), (2) C and CP violation, and (3) departure from thermal equilibrium. All three are necessary: B violation creates the asymmetry, C/CP violation ensures that the asymmetry is not canceled by equal and opposite processes, and out-of-equilibrium dynamics prevents the asymmetry from being washed out by inverse reactions.

[EST PHYS] In the Standard Model: (1) B violation occurs via electroweak sphalerons (Klinkhamer & Manton, 1984), which violate B+L by 6 units and conserve B-L. The sphaleron rate is Γ_sp ~ 10² T⁴ exp(-4π/α_W) at high temperatures (T > 10¹² GeV), where α_W is the weak coupling. At temperatures below the electroweak phase transition (T < 100 GeV), the sphaleron rate is exponentially suppressed, and B violation effectively freezes out. (2) C violation is present in the weak interaction via the V-A structure (only left-handed fermions and right-handed antifermions participate in weak interactions). CP violation is present in the CKM matrix via the Kobayashi-Maskawa phase (Kobayashi & Maskawa, 1973), but the SM CP violation is insufficient by ~10 orders of magnitude to explain the observed baryon asymmetry (Gavela et al., 1994; Huet & Sather, 1995). The Jarlskog invariant J ≈ 3 × 10⁻⁵ in the quark sector is too small. (3) Out-of-equilibrium dynamics could occur during a first-order electroweak phase transition, but the SM Higgs mass (m_H ≈ 125 GeV) is too large for a first-order phase transition in the SM (the phase transition is a smooth crossover, not a first-order transition). Thus, electroweak baryogenesis in the SM is ruled out.

[EST PHYS] Beyond the Standard Model (BSM) mechanisms for baryogenesis include: (1) leptogenesis (Fukugita & Yanagida, 1986): a lepton asymmetry is generated by the out-of-equilibrium decay of heavy right-handed neutrinos, and this lepton asymmetry is partially converted to baryon asymmetry via sphalerons. The CP violation in the PMNS matrix (the Dirac phase δ_CP and the Majorana phases) could provide the necessary CP violation. The heavy neutrino masses are typically M_N ~ 10⁹ - 10¹⁴ GeV. (2) Affleck-Dine mechanism (Affleck & Dine, 1985): a scalar field (e.g., a flat direction in the MSSM) acquires a large expectation value during inflation and decays out-of-equilibrium, generating a baryon asymmetry. (3) GUT baryogenesis: baryon number violation in grand unified theories (e.g., SU(5), SO(10)) generates a baryon asymmetry via the out-of-equilibrium decay of heavy gauge bosons or Higgs bosons. (4) Electroweak baryogenesis in extended models: adding a second Higgs doublet, a singlet scalar, or extra gauge bosons can restore the first-order phase transition and provide additional CP violation. (5) Spontaneous baryogenesis: a CP-violating background field (e.g., a pseudoscalar) couples to the baryon current and generates a baryon asymmetry in thermal equilibrium.

[SPEC] The neutrino sector may provide the missing CP violation. The PMNS matrix (for neutrino oscillations) has a Dirac phase δ_CP that is currently being measured by experiments like T2K, NOvA, and DUNE. The global fit to neutrino oscillation data gives δ_CP ≈ 230°-280° (with some uncertainty), and the CP-violating parameter J_PMNS ≈ 0.03-0.04. If neutrinos are Majorana particles, there are two additional Majorana phases that could contribute to CP violation. However, the CP violation in the PMNS matrix is not yet sufficient to explain the baryon asymmetry via standard leptogenesis — the heavy neutrino masses and couplings would need to be fine-tuned. Resonant leptogenesis (Pilaftsis, 1997) or flavored leptogenesis (Abada et al., 2006) could enhance the asymmetry, but these mechanisms require specific mass hierarchies and couplings.
