## 1. Epistemology of Measurement

### How Do We Know the CMB Exists and What It Tells Us?

[OBSERVED] The CMB was first detected by Penzias and Wilson in 1965 as an isotropic excess microwave noise at 7.35 cm wavelength, corresponding to T ≈ 3.5 K. They received the 1978 Nobel Prize in Physics for this discovery. The CMB was predicted by Gamow, Alpher, and Herman in 1948 as a consequence of the Big Bang model — a testable prediction that the Big Bang made decades before the CMB was observed. The COBE satellite (1989-1993) measured the blackbody spectrum to exquisite precision, confirming the thermal nature of the radiation and detecting the dipole anisotropy (ΔT/T ~ 10⁻³) due to the Earth's motion through the CMB rest frame. The FIRAS instrument on COBE measured the spectrum as a perfect blackbody with T = 2.725 ± 0.001 K (Mather et al., 1990; Fixsen et al., 1996).

[OBSERVED] The WMAP satellite (2001-2010) mapped the angular power spectrum of temperature anisotropies with resolution ~0.2°, revealing the first two acoustic peaks and measuring cosmological parameters: Ω_b h² ≈ 0.022, Ω_m h² ≈ 0.13, Ω_Λ ≈ 0.73, H_0 ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc, n_s ≈ 0.96. The Planck satellite (2009-2013) improved these measurements with resolution ~5 arcminutes, measuring the power spectrum to ℓ ~ 2500 and the CMB temperature to T = 2.72548 ± 0.00057 K (Planck Collaboration, 2018). The ACT and SPT ground-based experiments measure the CMB at small angular scales (ℓ ~ 3000-10000), providing complementary constraints on cosmological parameters.

[EST PHYS] The CMB is a snapshot of the universe at recombination (z ≈ 1100, t ≈ 380,000 years after the Big Bang). At this epoch, the universe cooled to T ≈ 3000 K, below which electrons and protons could combine to form neutral hydrogen. The mean free path of photons increased dramatically, and the photons decoupled from the baryonic matter. The CMB photons have been traveling freely ever since, redshifted by the cosmic expansion to microwave wavelengths today. The CMB is the most distant electromagnetic radiation we can observe, and it provides a direct probe of the early universe's physics.

[PHIL] **Theory-ladenness of observation:** The CMB is not "observed" directly — we observe voltage fluctuations in microwave detectors, calibrated against blackbody reference sources, and infer the CMB temperature from the angular power spectrum of these fluctuations. The inference that the CMB is a relic of the Big Bang depends on the Big Bang model itself. The CMB is a remarkable confirmation of the Big Bang, but the interpretation of its features (acoustic peaks, polarization, spectral distortions) depends on the theoretical framework of cosmological perturbation theory. The CMB is theory-laden, but the theory (Big Bang + inflation + ΛCDM) has been tested by many independent observations, including the CMB itself, the abundances of light elements (BBN), the expansion of the universe (Hubble law), and the large-scale structure of galaxies.

[EST PHYS] **Calibration chains and systematic errors:** The CMB temperature is measured through a chain of calibrations: (1) the FIRAS instrument on COBE was calibrated against a reference blackbody at liquid helium temperature, (2) the WMAP and Planck instruments are calibrated against the CMB dipole (which is a known anisotropy due to the Doppler effect from the Earth's motion), (3) the angular power spectrum is measured by cross-correlating detector outputs and subtracting foreground contamination (Galactic synchrotron, free-free, dust emission). Systematic errors include: beam shape uncertainties, detector gain drifts, foreground subtraction residuals, and polarization leakage. The Planck 2018 analysis addresses these systematics with extensive simulations and cross-checks between different frequency bands and detectors. The CMB temperature is known to ~0.02% precision, one of the most precisely measured quantities in cosmology.
