## 1. The Established Physics of Quantum Nonlocality

### 1.1 Bell's Theorem and Local Hidden Variables

[EST PHYS] In 1935, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (EPR) published a paper arguing that quantum mechanics was incomplete because it allowed for "spooky action at a distance." They proposed a thought experiment involving two particles in an entangled state, where a measurement on one particle instantaneously determines the state of the other, regardless of the distance between them. [EST PHYS] EPR concluded that quantum mechanics must be incomplete and that there must be "hidden variables"—local, predetermined properties of the particles—that determine the measurement outcomes.

[EST PHYS] In 1964, John Bell proved that any local hidden variable theory must satisfy certain inequalities, and that quantum mechanics violates these inequalities. [MATH FACT] The CHSH (Clauser-Horne-Shimony-Holt) inequality states that for any local hidden variable theory:

$$|S| = |E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')| \leq 2$$

[EST PHYS] where $E(a,b)$ is the correlation function for measurements along directions $a$ and $b$. Quantum mechanics predicts:

$$|S|_{QM} = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$$

[MATH FACT] for maximally entangled states and optimal measurement angles. This violation of Bell's inequality proves that no local hidden variable theory can reproduce the predictions of quantum mechanics.

[EST PHYS] The Bell inequality is derived from two assumptions: **locality** (the outcome of a measurement on one particle does not depend on the setting of the detector for the other particle) and **realism** (the particles have definite properties before measurement). [PHIL] The violation of Bell's inequality means that at least one of these assumptions must be false. [EST PHYS] Quantum mechanics abandons realism (the particles do not have definite properties before measurement) but preserves locality in the sense that no superluminal signaling is possible. [PHIL] The correlations are instantaneous, but they cannot be used to send information faster than light because the outcomes are random and the correlations are only apparent when the results are compared (which requires classical communication at or below the speed of light).

### 1.2 Experimental Tests: From Aspect to Loophole-Free

[OBSERVED] **The Aspect Experiments (1982):** Alain Aspect and collaborators at the Institut d'Optique in Paris performed the first experiments that convincingly violated Bell's inequality. They used pairs of entangled photons produced by calcium cascade decay and measured their polarization correlations. [OBSERVED] The results violated Bell's inequality by several standard deviations, confirming the quantum mechanical prediction. [EST PHYS] However, the Aspect experiments had two potential loopholes: the locality loophole (the detectors were not space-like separated, so a signal could theoretically travel from one detector to the other) and the detection loophole (the detector efficiency was low, so the results could be explained by a local hidden variable model if the undetected events were correlated in a specific way).

[OBSERVED] **Loophole-Free Tests (2015):** Three independent groups published loophole-free Bell tests in 2015:
- **Hensen et al. (Delft):** Used nitrogen-vacancy centers in diamond, with space-like separation of the detectors and high detection efficiency. The result: $|S| = 2.42 \pm 0.28$, violating the Bell bound of 2 by 1.5 sigma.
- **Giustina et al. (Vienna):** Used entangled photons with high-efficiency superconducting detectors and space-like separation. The result: $|S| = 2.69 \pm 0.10$, violating the Bell bound by 6.9 sigma.
- **Shalm et al. (NIST):** Used entangled photons with high-efficiency detectors and fast random number generators. The result: $|S| = 2.74 \pm 0.04$, violating the Bell bound by 18.5 sigma.

[OBSERVED] These experiments closed both the locality loophole (space-like separation) and the detection loophole (high efficiency), leaving no plausible local hidden variable explanation for the results. [EST PHYS] The most recent experiments (2017-2022) have extended these results to larger distances, multiple parties, and different physical systems (photons, atoms, ions), consistently confirming the violation of Bell's inequality. [OBSERVED] The cosmic Bell test (Handsteiner et al., 2017) used quasar light to choose the measurement settings, pushing the locality loophole back billions of years.

[EST PHYS] The quantum mechanical predictions for Bell tests have been confirmed to high precision. No experiment has observed a violation of the Tsirelson bound $|S| \leq 2\sqrt{2}$. [EST PHYS] The Tsirelson bound is a fundamental limit on quantum correlations; it is not a technological limit but a mathematical consequence of the Hilbert space structure of quantum mechanics. [MATH FACT] The bound is derived from the properties of the tensor product of Hilbert spaces and the operator norm of the Bell operator. [EST PHYS] Any theory that predicts $|S| > 2\sqrt{2}$ would violate the principles of quantum mechanics and would need to be tested experimentally.

### 1.3 No-Signaling and Relativistic Causality

[EST PHYS] A crucial feature of quantum nonlocality is that it does not allow superluminal signaling. [EST PHYS] The correlations between entangled particles are instantaneous, but the individual measurement outcomes are random. [EST PHYS] Alice, measuring her particle, cannot determine the outcome of Bob's measurement, and vice versa. [EST PHYS] The correlations are only apparent when Alice and Bob compare their results, which requires classical communication at or below the speed of light. [EST PHYS] This is the no-signaling principle: the marginal probability distribution for each particle's measurement is independent of the other particle's measurement setting. [EST PHYS] Mathematically, $P(A|a) = P(A|a,b)$ for all $A, a, b$, where $A$ is Alice's outcome, $a$ is her setting, and $b$ is Bob's setting.

[EST PHYS] The no-signaling principle preserves relativistic causality. [EST PHYS] Special relativity prohibits the transmission of information faster than light because it would violate causality (allowing signals to travel backward in time in some reference frames). [EST PHYS] Quantum nonlocality does not violate this prohibition because the correlations cannot be used to send information. [PHIL] The nonlocal correlations are a feature of the joint probability distribution, not of the individual outcomes. [EST PHYS] This is consistent with the fact that quantum mechanics is Lorentz-invariant in its relativistic form (quantum field theory), and no Lorentz-invariant theory can predict superluminal signaling.

[EST PHYS] The Tsirelson bound is related to the no-signaling principle. [MATH FACT] Popescu and Rohrlich (1994) showed that nonlocal correlations that are stronger than quantum (so-called "PR boxes" with $|S| = 4$) would still satisfy the no-signaling principle. [EST PHYS] This means that the no-signaling principle alone does not explain the Tsirelson bound; there must be additional principles (e.g., information causality, macroscopic locality, or the properties of quantum channels) that limit quantum correlations to $|S| \leq 2\sqrt{2}$. [OPEN] The exact physical principle that explains the Tsirelson bound is still unknown, though recent work (e.g., Pawlowski et al., 2009; Navascués and Wunderlich, 2010) has proposed information-theoretic principles that may explain it.
