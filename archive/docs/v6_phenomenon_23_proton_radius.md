## 1. The Experimental History of the Proton Radius

### 1.1 The Electronic Hydrogen Measurements (Historical)

[OBSERVED] Before 2010, the proton charge radius was measured primarily through two methods: (1) electron-proton scattering and (2) atomic hydrogen spectroscopy. [EST PHYS] Electron-proton scattering measures the electric form factor $G_E(Q^2)$ as a function of momentum transfer $Q^2$. The charge radius is extracted from the slope of $G_E$ at low $Q^2$: $r_p^2 = -6 \hbar^2 \left. \frac{dG_E}{dQ^2} \right|_{Q^2=0}$. [EST PHYS] Atomic hydrogen spectroscopy measures the energy difference between hydrogen levels (e.g., 2S-2P Lamb shift), which depends on the proton radius through the finite-size correction to the Coulomb potential.

[OBSERVED] The CODATA 2010 recommended value was $r_p = 0.8775(51)$ fm, based primarily on electron scattering data and atomic hydrogen spectroscopy. [EST PHYS] The uncertainty of ~0.06 fm was dominated by systematic errors in the extrapolation of the form factor to $Q^2 = 0$ and by the theoretical uncertainty in the atomic hydrogen Lamb shift calculation (particularly the two-photon exchange correction). [EST PHYS] Both methods are model-dependent: the scattering extraction requires an assumption about the functional form of $G_E(Q^2)$ at low $Q^2$, and the spectroscopic extraction depends on quantum electrodynamics (QED) calculations that include hadronic vacuum polarization and proton structure effects.

[PHIL] The epistemology of these measurements is subtle. We do not measure the proton radius directly; we measure scattering cross-sections or spectral line frequencies, and we *infer* the radius using theoretical models. [EST PHYS] The model dependence is significant: different functional forms for $G_E(Q^2)$ yield different radii, and different QED calculations yield slightly different finite-size corrections. [PHIL] The "radius" is not a directly observable quantity; it is a theoretical construct that parameterizes the spatial distribution of the proton's charge. [EST PHYS] The proton's charge distribution is not a hard sphere; it is a complex distribution determined by quark and gluon dynamics, with a tail that extends beyond the nominal radius. The "radius" is a characteristic length scale, not a physical boundary.

### 1.2 The Muonic Hydrogen Revolution (2010)

[OBSERVED] In 2010, the CREMA collaboration (Pohl et al.) published a measurement of the Lamb shift in muonic hydrogen ($\mu$p) using a laser spectroscopy technique at the Paul Scherrer Institute (PSI) in Switzerland. [EST PHYS] Muonic hydrogen is a bound state of a proton and a negative muon ($\mu^-$). The muon is ~207 times heavier than the electron, so the Bohr radius of muonic hydrogen is ~207 times smaller. [EST PHYS] Consequently, the finite-size correction to the Lamb shift is ~207$^3$ times larger than in electronic hydrogen, making the muonic hydrogen measurement vastly more sensitive to the proton radius.

[OBSERVED] The CREMA result was $r_p = 0.84184(67)$ fm—a stunning 4% discrepancy from the CODATA 2010 value of 0.8775 fm. [EST PHYS] The discrepancy corresponded to ~7-8 standard deviations, making it statistically impossible to attribute to random error. [HIST] This result became known as the "proton radius puzzle" and triggered an intense experimental and theoretical effort worldwide. [EST PHYS] Theoretical explanations proposed included: new physics beyond the Standard Model (e.g., a new force carrier coupling to muons), novel hadronic structure effects, and underestimated systematic errors in the electronic measurements.

[EST PHYS] The muonic hydrogen measurement is also model-dependent. The Lamb shift calculation requires QED corrections (vacuum polarization, self-energy, recoil corrections) and hadronic corrections (polarizability, two-photon exchange). [EST PHYS] The QED corrections for muonic hydrogen are larger than for electronic hydrogen because the muon is closer to the proton. However, the hadronic uncertainties are smaller because the finite-size effect dominates. [EST PHYS] The theoretical framework for muonic hydrogen is well-established, and the calculation has been verified by multiple independent groups. The dominant uncertainty in the muonic measurement is experimental (laser frequency, detector efficiency, etc.), not theoretical.

### 1.3 The Resolution: Convergence to ~0.84 fm (2019–2026)

[EST PHYS] Starting around 2019, new experimental results began to shift the consensus toward the muonic value. The key developments were:

[OBSERVED] **PRad Experiment (Jefferson Lab, 2019–2022):** The Proton Radius (PRad) experiment used a novel electron-proton scattering setup with a large-acceptance electromagnetic calorimeter and a hydrogen gas-flow target. The improved systematic control, particularly at low $Q^2$, yielded $r_p \approx 0.831$ fm, consistent with the muonic value. [EST PHYS] The PRad experiment reduced the model dependence of the low-$Q^2$ extrapolation by using a wider $Q^2$ range and a more constrained fitting procedure.

[OBSERVED] **Reanalysis of Historical Data:** Multiple groups (Horbatsch et al.; Zhou et al., 2025) used constrained Gaussian process regression and other modern statistical techniques to reanalyze historical electron scattering data. They found that previous extractions had underestimated systematic errors, particularly in the low-$Q^2$ extrapolation where the form factor shape is most uncertain. [EST PHYS] When these systematic errors were properly accounted for, the electronic scattering data were consistent with the muonic value.

[OBSERVED] **New Atomic Hydrogen Spectroscopy:**
- Grinin et al. (2021): Measured the 1S-3S transition in atomic hydrogen and obtained $r_p = 0.8482(38)$ fm.
- Bezginov et al. (2019): Measured the $n=2$ Lamb shift and obtained $r_p = 0.833(10)$ fm.
- Maisenbacher et al. (2026, *Nature*): Performed 2S-6P cryogenic hydrogen spectroscopy, achieving a precision that tested the Standard Model to 0.7 parts per trillion. Their result agreed with the muonic hydrogen value, effectively closing the proton radius puzzle. [EST PHYS]

[EST PHYS] The 2025 review by Xiong et al. concluded: *"Recent high-precision measurements of the proton charge radius using electron scattering and atomic hydrogen spectroscopy have produced results that align closely with the muonic hydrogen value, suggesting that earlier electron-based measurements may have contained unrecognized systematic errors."* The proton radius puzzle is now considered largely resolved.

[OBSERVED] **CODATA 2018/2022:** The CODATA recommended values have shifted toward the smaller radius. The exact recommended value continues to evolve as new measurements are incorporated, but the trend is unambiguous: the true proton charge radius is approximately **0.84 femtometers**.

[DISPROV] The convergence of all measurement methods toward a single value (~0.84 fm) directly contradicts the RHUFT claim that the proton has two different radii. [DISPROV] The two-radius interpretation was based on the premise that both the electronic and muonic measurements were correct but measured different physical quantities. [DISPROV] This premise is now known to be false: the earlier electronic measurements were systematically biased, and the correct radius is the smaller one. [DISPROV] The muonic measurement was more accurate from the beginning because the larger finite-size effect made it less sensitive to systematic errors in the theoretical model.
