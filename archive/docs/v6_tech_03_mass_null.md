## 1. Epistemology of Measurement: How Do We Know What Mass Is?

[PHIL] The question "What is mass?" is one of the deepest in physics. Newton defined mass as "quantity of matter" (quantitas materiae), measured by the product of density and volume. Einstein showed that mass is a form of energy (E = mc²), convertible to and from other forms. The Standard Model shows that mass arises from the Higgs mechanism, a dynamical symmetry-breaking process. General relativity shows that mass curves spacetime, and that this curvature is what we experience as gravity. Each of these frameworks answers the question differently, and none is fully reconciled with the others. The epistemology of mass measurement is therefore a study in the theory-ladenness of observation: what we measure depends on the theoretical framework we use.

[EST PHYS] The measurement of inertial mass relies on Newton's second law, F = ma. In practice, inertial mass is measured by comparing the acceleration of an unknown mass to the acceleration of a known mass (the International Prototype Kilogram, now replaced by the Planck-constant definition of the kilogram since 2019 [DEFINED]). The Kibble balance (formerly watt balance) measures mass by equating the gravitational force on a mass to the electromagnetic force on a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field [EST PHYS]. The precision of this measurement is now at the level of parts per billion, limited by systematic errors in the Planck constant determination, the gravitational acceleration g, and the electrical standards [OBSERVED]. This is a direct measurement, but it is theory-laden: it assumes that the gravitational force is proportional to mass (the equivalence principle), that electromagnetic forces are described by Maxwell's equations, and that the Planck constant is a fundamental constant of nature. If any of these theoretical assumptions were wrong, the measurement would be wrong — but it would still be internally consistent within the wrong theory.

[EST PHYS] The measurement of gravitational mass relies on the gravitational force law, F = Gm₁m₂/r². The Cavendish experiment (1797-1798) first measured the gravitational constant G by observing the torsion of a suspended rod attracted to massive lead spheres [HIST]. Modern measurements of G use torsion balances, atom interferometry, and satellite geodesy, achieving precisions of 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ [OBSERVED]. The disagreement between different G measurements (some differ by several standard deviations) is a notorious problem in precision physics [OPEN], suggesting that either systematic errors are underestimated or that the gravitational force law has small corrections not yet understood. The RHUFT framework notes this as an example of geometric "friction": the incommensurability between the measurement apparatus and the gravitational field creates a fundamental limit to measurement precision. This is a philosophical interpretation [PHIL], not a physics prediction.

[EST PHYS] The equivalence principle — the identity of inertial mass (m_i) and gravitational mass (m_g) — has been tested with extraordinary precision. Lunar laser ranging, which measures the relative acceleration of the Earth and Moon toward the Sun, has confirmed the equivalence principle to 10⁻¹³ [OBSERVED]. The MICROSCOPE satellite mission (2016-2018) tested the equivalence principle in space to 10⁻¹⁵ [OBSERVED].
