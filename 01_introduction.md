[EST PHYS] **Integrated Information Theory (IIT)**, developed by Giulio Tononi and collaborators, proposes that consciousness corresponds to a system's capacity for integrated information, quantified by the measure Φ ("phi," distinct from the golden ratio φ). [EST PHYS] IIT has been influential in neuroscience and has been applied to the study of brain states, anesthesia, and coma. [EST PHYS] However, IIT is controversial: it has been criticized for being unfalsifiable, for making counterintuitive predictions (e.g., that a grid of photodiodes can be conscious), and for lacking a clear physical mechanism. [SPEC]

[SPEC] RHUFT's coherence threshold $C(t) > \lambda = 1/\phi^2$ is conceptually analogous to IIT's Φ threshold, but it is not derived from IIT and differs in its mathematical formulation. [SPEC] Neither IIT nor RHUFT has been experimentally verified as a theory of consciousness. [SPEC] Both are speculative frameworks that attempt to formalize the relationship between information integration and subjective experience. [PHIL]

[PHIL] The hard problem of consciousness—why there is something it is like to be conscious—remains unsolved. [PHIL] RHUFT addresses this problem not by explaining it away but by proposing that awareness is as fundamental to the universe as space and time. [PHIL] This is a philosophical position, not a scientific claim. [PHIL]
